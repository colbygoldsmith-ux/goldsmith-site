About

I came up around finance. Then I chose the side that demands accountability.

I grew up just outside New York City, where success was measured in Wall Street terms, and I spent college at UC San Diego pointed straight at a career in finance. I was competitive enough to finish my degree in three years, and finance felt like the ultimate proving ground.

The closer I got, the less I liked what I saw: a culture that too often put profit ahead of people. By my senior year I knew I wanted the opposite: work grounded in integrity, where the job is to hold power accountable. I decided to become a lawyer, and I didn’t wait around to start.

Personal injury and medical malpractice are where I want to focus, and that’s personal. My brother is a physician in physical medicine and rehabilitation, the field that puts people back together after serious injury. His stories, and the negligence behind so many of them, from employers cutting corners to insurers and drivers who were never held to account, showed me exactly where I could do the most good.

Today I’m in San Francisco, where I moved with my partner, an OB nurse at UCSF. This August I begin law school at the University of San Francisco, with two years of hands-on exposure behind me and a clear sense of what I want to get good at.

My brother helps people recover. I want to make sure someone answers for the harm. The throughline

Experience

The foundation. Now to build on it.

The day I changed direction, I started cold-calling firms and leaning on the network I’d built running my own business. That persistence led me to litigation attorney David Kaufman, who gave me my first real foothold in the law. Those two years gave me what mattered most at this stage: a clear direction, and a close-up look at the craft I now want to spend law school learning to do well.
